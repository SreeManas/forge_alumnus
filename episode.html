Aerospace Career Beginner

The World of Aerospace

Sumanvitha KannamReddy · Collins Aerospace

Sumanvitha shares her journey into the aerospace industry, what daily life at Collins Aerospace looks like, and practical advice for students aspiring to build careers in aviation and defense technology. From engineering challenges to workplace culture — this episode covers it all.

Transcript

00:00 Hello and welcome to another episode of Talkiepedia! Today we have a very special guest, Sumanvitha KannamReddy from Collins Aerospace. Welcome, Sumanvitha!
00:15 Thank you so much for having me! I'm really excited to share my journey and hopefully inspire some of the viewers who are considering a career in aerospace.
00:35 Absolutely! So let's start from the beginning. How did you get into aerospace? Was it always your dream or did you discover it along the way?
00:52 Great question! Honestly, I didn't plan for aerospace specifically. I was always fascinated by engineering and problem-solving. During college, I got introduced to avionics through a workshop, and that changed everything.
01:22 That's fascinating. So what was the transition like from college to actually working at Collins Aerospace? Was there a big learning curve?
01:45 Absolutely, there was a huge learning curve. The thing about aerospace is that every component has to be perfect — lives depend on it. The attention to detail required is extraordinary compared to other industries.
02:20 Can you walk us through what a typical day looks like for you at Collins Aerospace?
02:35 Sure! I work on embedded systems for avionics. My day usually starts with a stand-up meeting, then I dive into design reviews, coding, and testing. The testing part is incredibly rigorous in aerospace — we have to certify everything against DO-178C standards.
03:15 For students watching this, what advice would you give to someone who wants to break into the aerospace industry?
03:30 First, build a strong foundation in engineering fundamentals. Don't just study for exams — understand the concepts deeply. Second, get involved in hands-on projects. Build drones, work on robotics clubs. Third, internships are crucial. Many aerospace companies hire from their intern pool.
04:20 That's brilliant advice. What about the work culture? Aerospace has a reputation for being very structured and process-heavy.
04:40 It is structured, but in a good way. When you're building systems that go into aircraft, you need that structure. But within the structure, there's incredible room for innovation. Collins is actually very forward-thinking when it comes to new technologies like AI and machine learning in avionics.
